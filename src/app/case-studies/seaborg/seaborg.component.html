The Project

Seaborg is a Danish startup seeking to design and manufacture a new type of nuclear reactor, environmentally friendly, that will help renewables in moving towards a future of low-carbon emissions. In doing so, they hope to change the current perception about nuclear energy. They needed a website and branding that reflected their values and mission.

The Background

Sustainable Nuclear Energy?

Yes, it sounds like an oxymoron, but, in fact, new nuclear is a key player in the fight agaisnt climate change. Most nuclear electricity comes from reactor designs that have not been updated since the 50s, and have well-known disadvantages. However, new types of reactors are being designed, which are more flexible, chaper, and most importantly, cleaner. Seaborg’s reactor is one of these.

Initial Research

It's not rocket science, or is it?

Communicating what Seaborg was doing was walking a very delicate political line. So, before deciding what to write on the website, I led a series of workshops on defining the mission and vision of the company, what audience segments we wanted to target, and what messages we wanted to push with the website. After a series of focused workshops, we sought feedback from the wider team, once key elements were put in place.

Forty nuclear scientists, in a room, thinking about branding.

Structure and Content

Building the blueprint

The second phase was a cycle of writing and defining the content of the website. The structure of the website was the result of card-sting and white boarding sessions. I created wireframes for the different pages and encouraged the COO to start writing the content in them rather than using separate word documents. I ruthlessly edited the writing into concise, clear sentences. Each section was then tested with members of the chosen audience groups, including Seaborg’s PR agency.

I wanted to stay visual as much as possible, and encouranged the communications and graphic designer of Seaborg to create diagrams and small illustrations to accompany the more technical explanations.

Visual Identity

Science doesn't have to be ugly

In parallel, I led ideation sessions for the visual design, we came up with several concepts, presented them to the team and one was chosen. I paired with Seaborg’s visual designer Susanne Husted to create a visual identity that was science-oriented, minimal and clean. A bold, graphic approach, was not suitable for the project, as flashy design could be detrimental to the carefully crafted technical message. The point was to inform and create trust, not to sell. My role was art directing, planning the story and flow of the website, and adapting the technical copy for the intended audience group.
